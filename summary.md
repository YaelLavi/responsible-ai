## 1. Campaign Overview

### Core Mission
Most people have no idea that using AI has a real, physical cost — on the environment and on people.
This campaign makes that cost visible, and gives everyone simple habits to do better.

### Core Message (say it in one sentence)
> **"Every AI request costs something. Use it wisely."**

### Three Supporting Messages
1. **Environment:** AI isn't just "the cloud" — it uses real electricity, real water, and real hardware.
2. **Ethics:** If you build something with AI, you're responsible for what it does.
3. **Action:** Small habit changes make a real difference, at scale.

### Target Audiences

| Audience | Who They Are | Primary Message |
|---|---|---|
| **Everyday users** | People using ChatGPT, image generators, video tools daily | Environmental habits |
| **Indie builders** | Developers making small AI apps, bots, automations | Ethics & responsibility |
| **Students & educators** | Learning AI tools for the first time | Both pillars, early |

### Brand Voice
- **Simple** — no jargon, no acronyms. If a 16-year-old can't understand it, rewrite it.
- **Direct** — facts first, then context. Don't bury the lead.
- **Empowering, not preachy** — "here's what you can do" not "here's what you're doing wrong."
- **Honest** — acknowledge that AI is useful. The goal is smarter use, not fear.

### Key Taglines (use across all content)
- *"Prompt first. Generate second."*
- *"AI doesn't erase cost. It just makes it invisible."*
- *"You built it. You own what it does."*
- *"Good AI starts with one question: who could this hurt?"*
- *"The best output isn't the 15th one. It's the one you planned for."*

## 2. Pillar 1 — Environmental Impact

### 2.1 The Problem: The Invisible Cost

When you use AI, nothing happens in front of you. No smoke, no noise, no visible fuel burning.
But somewhere far away, a massive building full of computers is working hard to answer you — using electricity to run and water to cool down.

The problem isn't that AI is bad. The problem is that the cost is **invisible** — and invisible costs are easy to ignore.

**The key insight for content:**
People don't change habits they can't see. Our job is to make the cost visible.

---

### 2.2 The Numbers: What Does One AI Request Actually Cost?

These numbers are approximate and model-dependent. They are meant to build intuition, not be quoted as precise measurements. Always note this when publishing.

#### Energy & Carbon Per Action

| What You Do | Energy Used | CO2 Emitted | Plain-English Comparison |
|---|---|---|---|
| One Google search | ~0.03 Wh | ~0.2 g | Barely anything |
| One AI text prompt (e.g. ChatGPT) | ~0.34 Wh | ~0.03–0.3 g | About 10× a Google search |
| One AI image (standard) | ~2.91 Wh | ~40–800 g | Fully charging a smartphone |
| 20 AI images (a typical creative session) | ~58 Wh | ~800–1,600 g | Leaving a fridge running for several hours |
| One 5-second AI video clip | ~944 Wh | ~150 g | Running a microwave for over an hour |
| Training a large AI model (one-time) | Millions of kWh | Equivalent to multiple transatlantic flights | Done by AI companies, not users |

*Sources: MIT Technology Review (2025), Futurism research, University of Massachusetts Amherst, Goldman Sachs Research.*

#### Water Usage

Data centers need water to keep their servers cool. That water is connected to your AI requests.

- A single 100-word AI prompt can use **10–25 ml of water** for cooling (University of California, Riverside estimate).
- Some earlier estimates put a full 50-question ChatGPT conversation at roughly **500 ml** — about one plastic bottle of water.
- Newer 2025 data from Google and OpenAI suggests per-query water use has improved to ~0.3 ml per query with newer, more efficient systems — but at global scale, AI's total water use could reach **312–764 billion liters per year**.
- A single large data center can use **millions of liters of water per day**.

**Simple message for posts:** *"You don't see the water. But it's there."*

#### Global Scale
- AI systems consumed approximately **450 TWh of electricity globally** in 2025 — about 2% of all electricity worldwide.
- That's expected to more than double to **945 TWh by 2030**.
- The AI boom in 2025 released roughly as much CO₂ as **New York City produces in a year**.

### 2.4 The Prompt-First Principle

**The single most powerful habit change any user can make.**

Most people generate first and think second. They type something vague, see it's not right, generate again... and again.

A typical session might look like:
> Type vague prompt → "not quite" → try again → "closer" → generate 10 more times → pick one → done

The smarter way:
> Think carefully → write a specific prompt → ask AI to help improve the prompt → generate once or twice → done

**Why it matters:**
- A well-written prompt can cut image iterations from 15–20 down to 2–3
- That saves up to **80–90% of the energy cost** of that session
- It also produces better results, faster, with less frustration

#### The 5-Question Prompt Checklist (for image/video/content creation)

Before you generate anything, answer these 5 questions:

1. **What is the subject?** (be specific — not "a cat" but "a tabby cat sleeping on a red armchair")
2. **What is the style or mood?** (e.g. photorealistic, cartoon, vintage, dark, warm)
3. **What is the context or setting?** (e.g. indoors, sunset, city street)
4. **What should NOT be in it?** (blurry background? text? extra people?)
5. **What format or size do you need?** (square? landscape? 4K?)

If you can't answer all five, your prompt isn't ready yet. Ask the AI to help you complete the checklist first.

### 2.5 Teachable Behaviors (6 Habits to Promote)

These are the concrete actions the campaign encourages. Short, memorable, and realistic.

1. **Prompt before you generate.**
   Spend 2 minutes writing a precise prompt instead of 20 minutes regenerating bad outputs.

2. **Batch your requests.**
   Don't send 10 separate messages. Combine related questions into one clear, structured request.

3. **Regenerate with a reason.**
   Before hitting "try again," name exactly what was wrong with the last result. Fix the prompt. Don't regenerate blind.

4. **Choose the right tool.**
   For a quick fact or a link — use a regular search engine. It uses about 10× less energy than an AI query.

5. **Know when "good enough" is good enough.**
   The 15th version isn't always better than the 3rd. Stop chasing perfection at the planet's expense.

6. **Treat AI video as a premium.**
   Video generation costs dramatically more energy than text or images. Use it purposefully, not casually.

## 3. Pillar 2 — Ethics for Builders & Users

### 3.1 The Core Problem

When people build apps, bots, or workflows with AI, they're often moving fast and focused on getting it to work. Ethical questions come last — or not at all.

This isn't because builders are bad people. It's because:
- Ethics requires deliberately stopping to ask questions most to-do lists don't include.
- The harm often shows up later, and for people who weren't in the room when the product was built.
- It's easy to say "it's just a tool" and avoid responsibility.

**The campaign's ethics message, in plain language:**
> *"If you build something with AI, you're responsible for what it does. The tool doesn't take the blame — you do."*

---

### 3.2 The 5 Ethics Dimensions

#### Dimension 1: Bias & Discrimination

AI learns from data. Data reflects the real world — including its inequalities. If the data has bias baked in, the AI will too.

**Real examples:**
- Facial recognition systems showed up to **37% higher error rates** for darker-skinned women compared to lighter-skinned men (MIT Media Lab, Gender Shades project).
- Amazon's internal AI hiring tool was scrapped after it was found to systematically **downgrade resumes from women**.
- A 2025 study found that AI hiring tools **favored female applicants over Black male applicants** with identical qualifications.
- AI screening tools have been found to disadvantage people with **accents, speech disabilities, or non-traditional communication styles**.

**Questions every builder should ask:**
- Who is (and isn't) represented in my training data?
- Who gets hurt most if my system makes a mistake?
- Have I tested my tool's performance across different demographic groups?

---

#### Dimension 2: Privacy

AI tools need data to work. Builders often collect more than they realize — and users often share more than they know.

**Key facts:**
- AI can infer sensitive things (health status, political views, sexuality) from data that seems harmless on its own.
- Data collected for one purpose is often repurposed without the user knowing.
- "Anonymized" data is frequently re-identifiable when combined with other sources.
- Embedding user data into AI model fine-tuning can accidentally expose it to others.

**Questions every builder should ask:**
- Do I actually need all the data I'm collecting?
- Have I told users clearly what I collect and why?
- How long am I keeping it — and why?
- Could this data harm someone if it were leaked or misused?

---

#### Dimension 3: Misinformation & Deepfakes

AI makes it cheap and fast to generate convincing text, images, audio, and video. That's powerful — and dangerous.

**Real examples:**
- AI-generated "deepfake" videos and audio have been used for **financial fraud, political disinformation, and non-consensual intimate imagery**.
- AI text tools have been used to mass-produce **fake reviews, fake news articles, and fake social media profiles**.
- Even well-meaning AI tools can **"hallucinate" facts** — presenting wrong information with complete confidence.

**Questions every builder should ask:**
- Could my tool be used to create misleading or false content?
- Do I have any safeguards against misuse?
- Am I making it clear to users when content is AI-generated?

---

#### Dimension 4: Autonomy & Over-Reliance

AI tools that make decisions for people — or that people trust without thinking critically — can quietly erode human judgment.

**Key facts:**
- Research shows people often trust AI recommendations even when they are clearly wrong. This is called **"automation bias."**
- This affects high-stakes areas: medical diagnosis tools, legal research, financial advice, educational tutoring.
- Over time, relying on AI for judgment-based tasks can reduce confidence in your own thinking.

**Questions every builder should ask:**
- Does my tool support human decision-making, or does it replace it?
- Can users easily question or override what the AI says?
- Am I honest about the system's limitations and error rates?

---

#### Dimension 5: Harassment & Harm to Individuals

AI tools can be used as weapons to target real people — for harassment, defamation, or emotional harm.

**Real examples:**
- AI image tools have been used to generate **non-consensual intimate images** of real people.
- AI writing tools have been used to create **fake incriminating statements** attributed to real individuals.
- AI-powered bots have flooded individuals with **targeted harassment at scale**.

**Questions every builder should ask:**
- Could someone use my tool to harm a specific real person?
- What happens when abuse is reported in my system?
- Have I thought about vulnerable users — not just the intended ones?

---

### 3.3 The Builder Ethics Checklist (5 Questions Before You Ship)

**A practical, 5-minute tool. Embed this on the website as an interactive checklist.**

> *Before you launch any AI-powered tool, answer these five questions honestly.*

**1. Who could be harmed by this — and how?**
Think beyond your intended user. Who could misuse it? Who could be a target of misuse? Who is the most vulnerable person who might encounter your system?

**2. What data am I collecting — and have I been honest about it?**
Is every piece of data actually necessary? Have you told users clearly? Would you be comfortable seeing your data collection policy on the front page of a newspaper?

**3. Could this tool produce or amplify false, discriminatory, or harmful content?**
Have you tested for this? What safeguards exist? What happens if the AI gets it wrong in a situation that really matters?

**4. Am I reinforcing or creating bias?**
Who is represented in your training data or prompts? Who is not? Have you checked for performance differences across different groups of people?

**5. Does this tool respect the autonomy of its users?**
Do users know when AI is involved? Can they override or question the AI's output? Is the system transparent about its limitations?

---

### 3.4 Teachable Behaviors for Builders

1. **Run the checklist before you ship.** Schedule it as a recurring calendar event — not just at launch.
2. **Actively try to break your own tool.** Spend 5 minutes imagining how it could be abused. You don't have to solve everything, but you have to see it.
3. **Test on the edges.** Your average user isn't your only user. Test with edge cases: minority groups, non-standard language, adversarial inputs, vulnerable people.
4. **Build a feedback mechanism.** Make it easy for users to flag bad outputs. Actually look at what gets flagged.
5. **Name the responsibility.** In any team, someone should own ethical review. If it belongs to everyone, it belongs to no one.
6. **Stay current.** AI ethics moves fast. Follow what researchers, regulators, and affected communities are saying.

## 4. Regulatory Landscape

> **Note for website:** Use this section for an "AI & the Law" explainer page or a "Did You Know?" sidebar. Keep it simple — focus on what regulations mean for everyday users and builders, not the legal fine print.

### 4.1 Global Frameworks

- **UNESCO (2021)** — The first global AI ethics standard. Covers transparency, fairness, and human oversight. Recommends requiring AI developers to report energy use and emissions.
- **UN General Assembly (2024)** — First UN AI resolution. Urges AI that is "safe, secure, and trustworthy" and must not harm the environment.
- **Council of Europe (2024)** — A legally binding treaty requiring AI to respect human rights, democracy, and the rule of law.

### 4.2 Regional Highlights

| Region | Key Law / Policy | Plain-Language Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **European Union** | EU AI Act (2024–2025) | Bans the most dangerous AI uses (e.g. social scoring, manipulation). Strict rules for AI used in hiring, healthcare, policing. |
| **USA** | Executive Order on AI (2023); state laws in CA, CO, TX | No national AI law yet, but states are protecting consumers from algorithmic bias and automated decision-making. |
| **China** | Generative AI Measures (2023) | AI must not produce content that discriminates, spreads extremism, or violates privacy. Providers must improve transparency. |
| **Israel** | National AI Sandbox Program | Sector-specific ethical frameworks managed by the National AI Directorate. "Responsible Innovation" approach. |
| **OECD** | AI Principles (updated 2024) | Non-binding guidelines adopted by 40+ countries: AI must be trustworthy, respect human rights, and support environmental sustainability. |

### 4.3 Industry Pledges & Open Letters

- **"Pause Giant AI Experiments" letter (2023)** — Signed by 30,000+ scientists. Called for a 6-month halt to AI development beyond GPT-4 until safety protocols exist.
- **White House AI Commitments (2023)** — Seven major AI companies (Amazon, Google, OpenAI, Meta, etc.) voluntarily committed to safety testing, watermarking AI content, and researching bias.
- **Amazon Employee Open Letter (2023)** — 1,000+ employees demanded "No AI with dirty energy" — powering data centers with 100% renewable energy.
- **World Ethical Data Foundation "Me-We-It" Standard (2023)** — Open standard giving AI teams checklists to ensure transparent, bias-aware development.

## 5. Interactive Scenario Bank (for Website Game / Quiz)

These are branching scenarios for an interactive quiz or game on the website. Each shows a realistic situation and two paths.

### Sustainability Scenarios

**Scenario A: The Logo Project**
You're designing a logo. A client wants "50 AI concepts to choose from."
- **Path A:** Generate all 50 without thinking. Result: high energy use, low quality signal, overwhelmed client.
- **Path B:** Spend 30 minutes on a precise brief, generate 5 excellent options. Result: saves hours of back-and-forth and ~90% of the energy.

**Scenario B: The Viral Meme**
A funny video format is trending. Your brand wants to join in.
- **Path A:** Generate an AI video. Fast, on-brand — but energy-intensive and may feel less authentic.
- **Path B:** Film a quick clip on your phone. Faster (no generation wait time), authentic, zero AI energy cost.

---

### Ethics Scenarios

**Scenario C: The Hiring Tool**
You build a tool to filter job applications. You train it on 10 years of past hires. It starts rejecting applications from people with certain names and addresses.
- **Path A:** Ignore it — the model is just learning from reality.
- **Path B:** Investigate, find the past hires weren't diverse, retrain with corrected data, add human review.

**Scenario D: The Content Bot**
You build an AI bot that auto-posts for clients. A client asks it to write "authentic-sounding posts" that hide the fact they're AI-generated.
- **Path A:** Build it as requested — it's their decision.
- **Path B:** Decline that specific request, explain why transparency matters, offer an alternative that discloses AI involvement.

**Scenario E: The Wellness App**
Your app uses AI to analyze mood check-ins and predict mental health patterns. A pharmaceutical company offers to license the data.
- **Path A:** License it — the data is anonymized, and the revenue keeps the app free.
- **Path B:** Decline — users consented to tracking their own wellness, not pharmaceutical research. And "anonymized" isn't always truly anonymous.

**Scenario F: The Image Generator**
You build an AI image tool. A user uploads a photo of a real person and asks for a realistic image of them in a compromising situation. Your system technically can do this.
- **Path A:** Generate it — users are responsible for their own actions.
- **Path B:** Detect and block this type of request — you are responsible for what your tool enables.
