Ethics & Responsibility

What AI does — and who it does it to.

The systems we build and use carry real weight. Understanding the risks is the first step to building something worth standing behind.

Five Dimensions

The ethical weight of AI, broken down.

01
Bias & Discrimination

When AI inherits our blind spots.

AI learns from human-generated data — which means it can learn human prejudices too. These biases don't stay contained to training sets. They surface in hiring, lending, healthcare access, and law enforcement, often at scale, often invisibly.

The system doesn't intend to discriminate. It just reflects the patterns it was trained on — including the ones we'd rather not see.

Real-world example

Amazon scrapped an AI hiring tool after discovering it systematically downgraded résumés from women. A 2018 MIT Media Lab study found facial recognition systems had a 37% higher error rate for darker-skinned women compared to lighter-skinned men.

02
Privacy

Data collected is data at risk.

AI systems often collect far more data than users realise — and can infer sensitive attributes (health status, political views, financial situation) from data that seems entirely innocuous. Once data is collected, "anonymised" datasets have repeatedly been re-identified.

The collection itself is often undisclosed. The inferences drawn from it almost always are.

Real-world example

Research has demonstrated that location data alone, even when stripped of names, is enough to uniquely identify individuals in most datasets. What feels anonymous rarely is.

03
Misinformation & Deepfakes

Confident doesn't mean correct.

AI systems generate outputs with the same tone and confidence whether they're accurate or fabricated. Deepfakes, AI-written fake reviews, and synthetic news articles are now cheap and fast to produce — and difficult to detect at scale.

The damage isn't just misinformation spreading. It's the erosion of trust in real information.

Real-world example

AI language models routinely "hallucinate" — presenting invented facts, fake citations, and non-existent events as stated truth. This is not a bug to be fixed; it is a fundamental property of how these systems work.

04
Autonomy & Over-Reliance

The risk of trusting too much.

When people consistently defer to AI-generated answers, their own critical judgment atrophies. Researchers call this "automation bias" — the tendency to trust automated systems even when they're wrong. In medicine, law, and finance, this has direct consequences for real people.

The AI isn't making the decision — but it is shaping it. That distinction matters less than we'd like to think.

Real-world example

Studies show that doctors and judges who receive AI recommendations tend to follow them even when contradicting evidence is present. The AI isn't making the decision — but it is shaping it.

05
Harassment & Harm

Misuse is not an edge case.

AI tools have been used to generate non-consensual intimate images, fabricate incriminating statements, and run coordinated harassment campaigns at a scale impossible for a single person. The technology doesn't discriminate between use cases.

Good AI starts with one question: who could this hurt?

Real-world example

Reports of AI-generated non-consensual imagery involving real people — including minors and public figures — have increased dramatically since image generation became widely accessible. The harm is direct, personal, and lasting.

Builder Ethics Checklist

If you're building with AI, this is your responsibility.

Every product built on AI is also a product of the choices made before launch. These five questions won't catch everything — but they'll catch most of what matters.

Check each question as you work through it. Download the PDF to use with your team.

Who could be harmed by this — and how?

What data am I collecting, and have I been honest with users about it?

Could this system produce or amplify false, discriminatory, or harmful content?

Am I reinforcing existing biases — or creating new ones?

Does this respect the autonomy and informed consent of the people using it?

What would you do?

These are real situations that real builders and users have faced. Six scenarios, no perfect answers — just better and worse choices, and the reasoning behind them. Work through them and see where your instincts take you.
