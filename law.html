Global Frameworks

From guidelines to binding law.

AI regulation moved from theoretical to enforceable between 2021 and 2024. Governments and international bodies aren't waiting for the industry to self-regulate — they're writing binding rules, setting penalties, and establishing rights. Whether you use AI or build with it, this affects you.

Europe · 2024

EU AI Act

The world's most comprehensive AI law. The EU AI Act categorises AI systems by risk level — banning the highest-risk uses outright and imposing strict obligations on "high-risk" applications in areas like hiring, education, and law enforcement. Providers must be transparent about how their systems work.

Key implication

Any AI system made available in the EU — regardless of where it was built — must comply.

International · 2021

UNESCO Recommendation on AI Ethics

The first global standard on AI ethics, adopted by all 193 UNESCO member states. It covers fairness, transparency, accountability, and — uniquely — environmental sustainability. It recommends that governments require AI developers to disclose energy use and carbon emissions.

Key implication

Governments who signed are expected to put national AI ethics frameworks in place.

International · 2024

UN General Assembly Resolution

The first ever UN resolution on AI, passed in March 2024. It affirms that AI must be safe, trustworthy, and rights-respecting — and explicitly states that AI systems must not cause environmental harm.

Key implication

Sets a global normative baseline that national governments are expected to align with.

Europe · 2024

Council of Europe AI Treaty

The first legally binding international treaty on AI. Signed by Council of Europe member states plus the US, UK, and others, it requires signatories to ensure AI systems respect human rights, democracy, and the rule of law — with enforceable obligations, not just guidelines.

Key implication

Unlike recommendations, this is a binding treaty — governments that sign must bring their laws into compliance.

40+ Countries · Updated 2024

OECD AI Principles

Adopted by over 40 countries, the OECD Principles define what "trustworthy AI" looks like in practice: transparent, explainable, robust, secure, and accountable. The 2024 update added explicit requirements around environmental sustainability.

Key implication

These principles shape national policy across most of the world's largest economies.

United States · 2023

US Executive Order on AI

President Biden's Executive Order on "Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI" directed federal agencies to develop AI safety standards and required major AI companies to share safety testing results with the government before public release. It also prompted voluntary commitments from leading AI companies.

Key implication

Sets a federal benchmark for AI safety in the US, though enforcement depends on successive administrations.

China · 2023

China Generative AI Regulations

China's rules for generative AI — effective August 2023 — require that AI-generated content be clearly labelled, that systems not produce discriminatory or false content, and that training data meet legal standards. Providers must register with regulators before launch.

Key implication

One of the first national frameworks specifically targeting generative AI products like chatbots and image generators.

Israel · Ongoing

Israel National AI Framework

Israel's approach centres on a National AI Sandbox Program — allowing controlled testing of AI innovations under regulatory oversight — alongside sector-specific ethics frameworks for healthcare, legal, and financial applications. The goal is innovation with guardrails, rather than blanket regulation.

Key implication

Demonstrates a risk-proportionate regulatory model that other countries are watching closely.

What This Means For You

Regulation isn't abstract. It applies to you.

For Users

  • You have growing rights around AI decisions that affect you — including the right to know when AI was involved.
  • In the EU, you can challenge automated decisions made about you (hiring, credit, insurance) and request human review.
  • Labelling requirements mean AI-generated content should increasingly be disclosed — look for it.

For Builders

  • If your product touches the EU market, the AI Act applies regardless of where you're based.
  • Transparency is no longer optional: document your training data, disclose AI involvement, and be ready to explain your system's decisions.
  • Environmental accountability is entering regulation — now is the time to measure and reduce your system's energy footprint, before it becomes mandatory.
